Black holes are formed when a massive star runs out of fuel and collapses under its own gravity. As the star collapses, its density increases, and the gravitational force becomes stronger. If the mass of the star is large enough, the gravitational force becomes so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from it. This point of no return is called the event horizon, and it marks the boundary of the black hole.

The size of a black hole is determined by the mass of the star that formed it. The larger the star, the larger the black hole. Black holes can also be formed by the collision of two neutron stars or by the collapse of a massive gas cloud.

Once a black hole is formed, it continues to grow as it absorbs matter from its surroundings. The matter that falls into a black hole is compressed and heated to extreme temperatures, emitting X-rays and other forms of radiation. Black holes are invisible, but their presence can be detected by observing the effects of their gravity on nearby objects.
