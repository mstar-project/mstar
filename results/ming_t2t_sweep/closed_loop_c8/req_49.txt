"1984" is a dystopian novel by George Orwell, published in 1949. The story is set in a totalitarian society ruled by the Party, led by Big Brother, where individuality and independent thought are suppressed. The protagonist, Winston Smith, works at the Ministry of Truth, where he alters historical records to fit the Party's propaganda.

Winston becomes disillusioned with the oppressive regime and begins a forbidden affair with Julia, a coworker. They seek out a secret resistance group called the Brotherhood, led by a man named O'Brien. However, they are betrayed and captured by the Thought Police.

Winston is subjected to intense torture and brainwashing in the Ministry of Love, where he is forced to betray Julia and accept the Party's ideology. In the end, Winston is broken and indoctrinated, accepting the Party's control over his thoughts and actions.

The novel explores themes of government surveillance, censorship, and the dangers of totalitarianism. It also highlights the importance of individual freedom and the human spirit's resilience against oppression.
