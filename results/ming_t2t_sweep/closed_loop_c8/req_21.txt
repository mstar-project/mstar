Occam's Razor is a principle that suggests that when presented with competing hypotheses or explanations, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected. In other words, the simplest explanation is usually the best one. This principle is named after William of Ockham, a 14th-century English philosopher and theologian who popularized it. The idea is that if two theories can explain the same phenomenon, the simpler one is more likely to be correct. However, it's important to note that Occam's Razor is not a rule of logic, but rather a heuristic or guideline for choosing between competing explanations. It's also worth noting that sometimes the simplest explanation may not be the correct one, and more complex explanations may be necessary to fully understand a phenomenon.
