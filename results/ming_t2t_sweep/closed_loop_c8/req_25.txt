The five stages of grief, as proposed by Elisabeth Kübler-Ross in her 1969 book "On Death and Dying," are:

1. **Denial**: This is the initial stage where a person may refuse to accept the reality of the loss. They might feel numb or disbelieving, and may even deny that the loss has occurred.

2. **Anger**: In this stage, the person may feel intense anger or frustration. This can be directed at themselves, others, or even at the situation or the person who has died. Anger can also manifest as irritability or resentment.

3. **Bargaining**: During this stage, a person may try to negotiate with a higher power or fate to reverse the loss. They might make promises or deals in an attempt to change the outcome.

4. **Depression**: This stage involves deep sadness and despair. The person may feel overwhelmed by their emotions and may withdraw from others. They might experience feelings of hopelessness and may struggle with daily activities.
