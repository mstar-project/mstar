The Rosetta Stone is a granodiorite stele inscribed with a decree issued in Memphis, Egypt, in 196 BC on behalf of King Ptolemy V. The decree appears in three scripts: the top and middle texts are in Ancient Egyptian using hieroglyphic script and Demotic script, respectively, while the bottom is in Ancient Greek. Because it presents essentially the same text in all three scripts (with some minor differences between them), it provided the key to the modern understanding of Egyptian hieroglyphs.

The stone was discovered by French soldiers in 1799 during Napoleon's campaign in Egypt. It was taken to Britain after the defeat of Napoleon and is now housed in the British Museum in London. The stone has been a source of fascination for scholars and the public alike, and its decipherment has had a profound impact on our understanding of ancient Egypt.

The significance of the Rosetta Stone lies in its role in the decipherment of Egyptian hieroglyphs. Prior to its discovery, hieroglyphs were largely a mystery, and their meaning was unknown.
