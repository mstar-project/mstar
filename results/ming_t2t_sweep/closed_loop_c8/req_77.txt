The Fermi Paradox is a question that asks why, given the vast number of stars in the universe and the high probability of planets capable of supporting life, we have not yet found any evidence of extraterrestrial civilizations. The paradox was named after physicist Enrico Fermi, who reportedly asked the question during a lunch discussion with colleagues in 1950.

The paradox arises from the fact that if there are many potentially habitable planets in the galaxy, and if life can arise on some of them, then it seems likely that at least some of those planets would have developed intelligent life capable of communicating with us. However, despite decades of searching, we have not found any definitive evidence of such civilizations.

There are several possible explanations for the Fermi Paradox, including the possibility that intelligent life is rare or short-lived, that advanced civilizations may be avoiding contact with us, or that we simply haven't been looking in the right places or using the right methods to detect signs of extraterrestrial intelligence. The paradox remains an open question in the field of astrobiology and continues to inspire scientific inquiry and speculation.
