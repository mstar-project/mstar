Black holes are formed when a massive star runs out of fuel and collapses under its own gravity. As the star collapses, its density increases, and the gravitational force becomes stronger. If the mass of the star is large enough, the gravitational force becomes so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from it. This point of no return is called the event horizon, and it marks the boundary of the black hole.

The size of a black hole is determined by the mass of the star that formed it. The larger the star, the larger the black hole. There are three main types of black holes: stellar black holes, which are formed from the collapse of massive stars; intermediate black holes, which have masses between 100 and 100,000 times that of the sun; and supermassive black holes, which have masses millions to billions of times that of the sun and are found at the centers of galaxies.

In addition to the collapse of massive stars, black holes can also be formed through the merger of two smaller black holes or through the accretion of matter onto an existing black hole.
