Bread rises due to the action of yeast. Yeast is a type of fungus that feeds on sugars and produces carbon dioxide gas as a byproduct. When you mix yeast with flour, water, and other ingredients to make dough, the yeast begins to consume the sugars in the flour and produce carbon dioxide gas.

As the dough rests and rises, the carbon dioxide gas gets trapped in the gluten network of the dough, causing it to expand and rise. The gluten network is formed by the proteins in the flour, which stretch and trap the gas bubbles as the dough rises.

When you bake the bread, the heat causes the yeast to die off and the gluten to set, which helps to hold the structure of the bread and prevent it from collapsing. The carbon dioxide gas that was produced during the rising process also expands in the heat, helping to create the airy texture of the bread.

In addition to yeast, some breads are leavened with baking powder or baking soda, which also produce carbon dioxide gas when they react with moisture and heat. However, these chemical leaveners do not require the same amount of time to rise as yeast-based breads.
