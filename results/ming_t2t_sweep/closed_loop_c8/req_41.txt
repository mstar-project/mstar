The mitochondria is an organelle found in most eukaryotic cells and is often referred to as the "powerhouse" of the cell. Its primary role is to generate energy for the cell through a process called cellular respiration. During cellular respiration, mitochondria convert nutrients such as glucose into adenosine triphosphate (ATP), which is the main source of energy for the cell. ATP is used to power various cellular processes, including muscle contraction, protein synthesis, and active transport of molecules across cell membranes. In addition to energy production, mitochondria also play a role in other cellular functions such as calcium storage, apoptosis (programmed cell death), and heat production.
