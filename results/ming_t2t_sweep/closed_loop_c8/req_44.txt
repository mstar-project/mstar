Moore's Law is an observation made by Gordon Moore, co-founder of Intel, in 1965. It states that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles approximately every two years, leading to exponential growth in computing power and a corresponding decrease in cost. This trend has continued for several decades, driving advancements in technology and enabling the development of smaller, faster, and more powerful electronic devices. However, it's important to note that Moore's Law is not a physical law but rather an empirical observation that has held true for a significant period of time. As of recent years, the pace of transistor density increase has slowed down, and the industry is exploring new ways to continue advancing technology.
