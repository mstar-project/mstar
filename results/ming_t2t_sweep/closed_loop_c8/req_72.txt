Copyrights and trademarks are both forms of intellectual property protection, but they protect different types of creations and serve different purposes.

A copyright protects original works of authorship, such as literary, musical, artistic, and other creative works. It gives the creator of the work exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, perform, display, and create derivative works based on the original. Copyright protection arises automatically upon creation of the work and lasts for the life of the author plus 70 years.

A trademark, on the other hand, protects brand names, logos, slogans, and other distinctive signs used in commerce to identify and distinguish goods or services from those of others. A trademark can be registered with the government to provide additional legal protections, such as the exclusive right to use the mark in connection with specific goods or services. Trademark protection can last indefinitely as long as the mark is in use and its registration is renewed periodically.

In summary, copyrights protect creative works, while trademarks protect brand identity and commercial symbols.
