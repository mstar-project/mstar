The time complexity of quicksort can vary depending on the specific implementation and the input data. However, in general, the average-case time complexity of quicksort is O(n log n), where n is the number of elements in the array being sorted. This is because quicksort uses a divide-and-conquer approach, where the array is recursively divided into smaller sub-arrays until each sub-array contains only one element, which is then merged back together in sorted order.

In the worst-case scenario, where the pivot chosen for each partition is the smallest or largest element in the sub-array, the time complexity of quicksort can degrade to O(n^2). However, this can be mitigated by using techniques such as randomization or choosing the median of three elements as the pivot.

Overall, quicksort is considered to be one of the most efficient sorting algorithms for large datasets, due to its average-case time complexity of O(n log n) and its ability to sort in-place without requiring additional memory.
