CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) is a revolutionary gene-editing technology that has transformed the field of genetics and biotechnology. It's a powerful tool that allows scientists to edit genes with unprecedented precision, efficiency, and flexibility. Here's a simplified explanation of what CRISPR is and how it works:

**What is CRISPR?**

CRISPR is a natural defense mechanism found in bacteria and other microorganisms. It's a system that protects them from viral infections by recognizing and destroying foreign genetic material. Scientists have harnessed this natural mechanism to develop a gene-editing tool that can be programmed to target specific DNA sequences.

**How does CRISPR work?**

The CRISPR-Cas9 system consists of two main components:

1. **Guide RNA (gRNA)**: A small piece of RNA that is programmed to recognize a specific DNA sequence. The gRNA is designed to bind to a particular location on the genome, much like a lock and key.
2. **Cas9 enzyme**: An enzyme that acts as molecular scissors, cutting the DNA at the targeted location.
