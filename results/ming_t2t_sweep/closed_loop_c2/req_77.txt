The Fermi Paradox is a question that asks why, given the vast number of stars in the universe and the high probability of planets capable of supporting life, we have not yet found any evidence of extraterrestrial civilizations. The paradox was named after physicist Enrico Fermi, who, in 1950, asked his colleagues at Los Alamos National Laboratory, "Where is everybody?"

The paradox arises from the fact that, based on our current understanding of the universe, there should be many more intelligent civilizations out there than we have observed. The Drake Equation, developed by astronomer Frank Drake in 1961, attempts to estimate the number of active, communicative extraterrestrial civilizations in the Milky Way galaxy. The equation takes into account factors such as the rate of star formation, the fraction of stars with planets, the number of planets that could potentially support life, and the fraction of those planets that actually develop life.

Despite the high probability of extraterrestrial life, we have not yet detected any signals or signs of intelligent life beyond Earth. This has led to various hypotheses to explain the paradox, including the possibility that advanced civilizations are rare, that they self-destruct before they can communicate with us, or that they are deliberately avoiding contact with us
