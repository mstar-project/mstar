Black holes are formed when a massive star runs out of fuel and collapses under its own gravity. As the star collapses, its density increases, and the gravitational force becomes stronger. If the mass of the star is large enough, the gravitational force becomes so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from it. This point of no return is called the event horizon, and it marks the boundary of the black hole.

The size of a black hole is determined by the mass of the star that formed it. The larger the star, the larger the black hole. Black holes can also form when two neutron stars or a neutron star and a black hole merge.

It's important to note that black holes are invisible, as they don't emit any light or radiation. However, astronomers can detect them by observing the effects of their gravity on nearby matter, such as stars and gas clouds.
