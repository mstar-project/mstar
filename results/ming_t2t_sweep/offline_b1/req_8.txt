Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy in the form of glucose. It occurs in two stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent reactions (also known as the Calvin cycle).

In the light-dependent reactions, light energy is absorbed by pigments called chlorophylls and other accessory pigments in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts. This energy is used to split water molecules into oxygen gas, protons, and electrons. The electrons are then passed through a series of proteins in the thylakoid membrane, generating a proton gradient that drives the synthesis of ATP (adenosine triphosphate) and NADPH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate), which are energy-rich molecules.

In the light-independent reactions, also known as the Calvin cycle, the ATP and NADPH produced in the light-dependent reactions are used to fix carbon dioxide from the atmosphere into organic molecules. This process involves a series of enzyme-catalyzed reactions that ultimately produce glucose and other sugars. The glucose can then be used by the plant for energy or stored as starch for later use.
