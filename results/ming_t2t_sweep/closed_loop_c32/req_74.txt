A search engine indexes the web by using automated programs called "spiders" or "crawlers" to systematically browse and analyze websites. These programs follow links from one page to another, collecting information about the content and structure of each page they visit. 

The information collected by the spiders includes the text on the page, the HTML code, images, videos, and other multimedia content. The spiders also record the URL of each page they visit, as well as any metadata associated with the page, such as the title, description, and keywords.

Once the spiders have collected this information, it is stored in a massive database that the search engine can search through when a user enters a query. The search engine uses complex algorithms to analyze the information in its database and determine which pages are most relevant to the user's query.

The search engine then ranks the results based on a variety of factors, including the relevance of the page to the query, the quality of the content, the authority of the website, and the user experience. The results are then displayed to the user in a list, with the most relevant and authoritative pages appearing at the top of the list.
