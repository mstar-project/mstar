Moore's Law is the observation that the number of transistors in a dense integrated circuit doubles approximately every two years. This trend has continued for more than 50 years, and it is expected to continue for the foreseeable future. The law is named after Gordon Moore, who co-founded Intel Corporation and is one of the founders of the semiconductor industry.

Moore's Law is not a physical law, but rather an empirical observation that has held true for several decades. It has been a driving force behind the rapid advancement of technology, as it has enabled the creation of smaller, faster, and more powerful electronic devices. However, as the size of transistors approaches the atomic scale, it is becoming increasingly difficult to maintain the pace of Moore's Law, and some experts predict that it will eventually reach its limits.
