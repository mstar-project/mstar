The five stages of grief, as proposed by Elisabeth Kübler-Ross in her 1969 book "On Death and Dying," are:

1. **Denial**: This is the initial stage where a person may refuse to accept the reality of the loss. They might feel numb or disbelieving, and may even deny that the loss has occurred.

2. **Anger**: In this stage, the person may feel intense anger or frustration. They might direct this anger at themselves, others, or even at the situation or the person who has died.

3. **Bargaining**: During this stage, a person may try to negotiate with a higher power or fate in an attempt to reverse or lessen the loss. They might make promises or deals in exchange for more time or a different outcome.

4. **Depression**: This stage involves deep sadness and despair. The person may feel overwhelmed by the loss and may withdraw from others or experience a sense of hopelessness.

5. **Acceptance**: In the final stage, the person begins to come to terms with the loss. They start to adjust to life without the person or thing they have lost and begin to move forward.
