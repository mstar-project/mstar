Black holes are formed when a massive star runs out of fuel and collapses under its own gravity. As the star collapses, its density increases, and the gravitational force becomes stronger. If the mass of the star is large enough, the gravitational force becomes so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from it. This point of no return is called the event horizon, and it marks the boundary of the black hole.

The size of a black hole is determined by the mass of the star that formed it. The larger the mass of the star, the larger the black hole will be. Black holes can range in size from just a few miles across to billions of times the mass of our sun.

In addition to the collapse of massive stars, black holes can also form through the collision of two neutron stars or the direct collapse of a massive gas cloud. These processes can result in the formation of intermediate-mass black holes or supermassive black holes, which are found at the centers of galaxies.
