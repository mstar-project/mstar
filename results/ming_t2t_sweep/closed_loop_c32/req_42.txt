Bread rises due to the action of yeast. Yeast is a type of fungus that feeds on sugars and produces carbon dioxide gas as a byproduct. When you mix yeast with flour, water, and other ingredients to make dough, the yeast begins to consume the sugars in the flour and produce carbon dioxide gas.

As the dough rests and rises, the carbon dioxide gas gets trapped in the gluten network of the dough, causing it to expand and rise. The gluten network is formed by the proteins in the flour, which stretch and trap the gas bubbles as the dough rises.

When you bake the bread, the heat causes the yeast to die off, but the carbon dioxide gas that was produced during rising remains trapped in the dough. This causes the bread to continue to rise as it bakes, resulting in a light and airy texture.

In addition to yeast, some breads also use baking powder or baking soda as a leavening agent. These chemicals react with acidic ingredients in the dough to produce carbon dioxide gas, which also helps the bread to rise. However, yeast is the most common leavening agent used in bread making.
