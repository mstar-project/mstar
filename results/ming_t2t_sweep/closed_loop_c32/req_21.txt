Occam's Razor is a principle that suggests that when presented with competing hypotheses or explanations, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected. In other words, the simplest explanation is usually the best one. This principle is named after William of Ockham, a 14th-century English philosopher and theologian who popularized it.

The idea behind Occam's Razor is that unnecessary complexity should be avoided in explanations. If two theories can explain the same phenomenon equally well, the simpler one is preferred because it requires fewer assumptions and is therefore less likely to be wrong. However, it's important to note that Occam's Razor is not a rule that must always be followed; sometimes, a more complex explanation may be necessary to account for all the available evidence.
