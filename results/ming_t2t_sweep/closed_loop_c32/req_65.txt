The Electoral College is a process established by the United States Constitution for electing the President and Vice President of the United States. Here's how it works:

1. **Number of Electors**: Each state has a number of electors equal to the total number of its Senators and Representatives in Congress. The District of Columbia has three electors, as if it were a state. This means there are 538 electors in total (435 Representatives + 100 Senators + 3 for D.C.).

2. **Winning the Election**: To win the presidency, a candidate must receive at least 270 electoral votes (a majority of the 538).

3. **State-by-State Voting**: When citizens vote for president, they are actually voting for a slate of electors pledged to their chosen candidate. These electors are typically selected by the political parties in each state.
