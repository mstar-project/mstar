The Fermi Paradox is a question that asks why, given the vast number of stars in the universe and the high probability of planets with conditions suitable for life, we have not yet found any evidence of extraterrestrial civilizations. The paradox is named after physicist Enrico Fermi, who reportedly asked, "Where is everybody?" during a discussion about the possibility of extraterrestrial life.

The paradox arises from the fact that if there are many potentially habitable planets in the universe, it seems likely that some of them would have developed intelligent life capable of interstellar communication or travel. However, despite extensive searches for signs of extraterrestrial intelligence, such as radio signals or other forms of technology, no definitive evidence has been found.

There are several possible explanations for the Fermi Paradox, including the idea that intelligent life may be rare or short-lived, that advanced civilizations may be avoiding contact with us, or that we may simply not be looking in the right places or at the right times. The paradox remains an open question in the field of astrobiology and continues to inspire scientific inquiry and speculation.
