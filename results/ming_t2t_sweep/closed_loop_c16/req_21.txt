Occam's Razor is a principle that suggests that when presented with competing hypotheses or explanations, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected. In other words, the simplest explanation is usually the best one. This principle is named after William of Ockham, a 14th-century English philosopher and theologian who popularized it. The idea is that, all else being equal, simpler explanations are more likely to be true than complex ones. This principle is often used in science, philosophy, and other fields to guide the development of theories and models.
