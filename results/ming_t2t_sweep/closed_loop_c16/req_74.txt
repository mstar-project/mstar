A search engine indexes the web by using automated programs called "spiders" or "crawlers" to scan and analyze web pages. These programs follow links from one page to another, collecting information about the content of each page they visit. The information collected includes the text on the page, the page's title, meta tags, headings, and other important elements.

Once the spider has collected this information, it sends it back to the search engine's servers, where it is stored in a massive database. The search engine then uses complex algorithms to analyze the data and determine the relevance of each page to specific search queries. This process is known as indexing.

The search engine's algorithms take into account a variety of factors when determining the relevance of a page, including the page's content, its popularity (as measured by the number of links pointing to it), and its authority (as determined by the quality of those links). The search engine also considers the user's search history, location, and other factors to provide personalized search results.
