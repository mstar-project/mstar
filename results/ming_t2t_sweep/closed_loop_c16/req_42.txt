Bread rises due to the action of yeast. Yeast is a type of fungus that feeds on sugars and produces carbon dioxide gas as a byproduct. When you mix yeast with flour, water, and other ingredients to make dough, the yeast begins to consume the sugars in the flour and produce carbon dioxide gas.

As the dough rests, the carbon dioxide gas gets trapped in the gluten network of the dough, causing it to expand and rise. This process is called fermentation. The longer the dough ferments, the more gas is produced, and the more the dough will rise.

When you bake the bread, the heat causes the gas to expand even further, creating the characteristic airy texture of the bread. At the same time, the heat also kills the yeast, stopping the fermentation process and setting the structure of the bread.

In addition to yeast, some breads are leavened with baking powder or baking soda, which also produce carbon dioxide gas when they react with moisture and heat. However, these chemical leaveners do not require the long fermentation time that yeast does, and they do not contribute to the complex flavor and texture of yeast-leavened breads.
